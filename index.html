Women's Health Product Judgment

Data trust · inclusive design · clinical credibility · monetization ethics — the judgment calls that define this category

1 — Health Data Trust & Regulatory Judgment

The single most-tested topic in women's-health PM interviews. Know the mechanism, not just the headline.

The case you must know: Flo Health, FTC settlement (2021)
Flo told users it wouldn't share health data, then shared cycle and pregnancy data with Facebook, Google, and other marketing/analytics SDKs. The FTC settlement required independent privacy audits for 20 years. The root cause was third-party SDKs receiving sensitive signals the product team hadn't fully audited — this is the case every interviewer in this category expects you to reference.

Best Practices

  • Audit every analytics/ad SDK for exactly what data it receives — treat this as a recurring review, not a one-time check
  • Purpose-limited consent — don't bundle health-data consent into generic ToS acceptance
  • Avoid diagnostic language ("detects," "diagnoses") that risks crossing into FDA-regulated medical device territory
  • Data minimization — collect only what a feature needs; set deletion schedules
  • Independent privacy review before shipping any new data-sharing integration

Vocabulary

  • SaMD — Software as a Medical Device (FDA classification)
  • FTC Health Breach Notification Rule — requires notice when health apps expose data
  • Purpose limitation — data used only for the reason it was collected
  • De-identification — removing info that could re-identify a user
  • PHI — Protected Health Information (a HIPAA term; mostly doesn't cover consumer health apps directly, but interviewers expect you to know why it's still relevant context)

Practice question: "A marketing team wants to add a new attribution SDK. How do you evaluate whether it's safe to ship in a cycle-tracking app?" Answer with a repeatable process — what data it touches, whether it's health-adjacent, what gets disclosed, what the opt-out looks like — not a gut call.

2 — Inclusive & Diverse User Design

An ICP defined as "35–50, regular cycle" is a real interview vulnerability if it's the whole story.

Segments Often Missed

  • Irregular cycles / PCOS — prediction models break when the input isn't periodic
  • Perimenopause & menopause — the fastest-growing segment in the category (Midi Health, Elektra Health, Stella all built around it)
  • Postpartum — an entirely different hormonal baseline
  • Trans and non-binary users — Clue's 2019 redesign removed gendered defaults from the core experience
  • Teens / first-cycle users — different privacy needs and often a shared device

Vocabulary

  • Amenorrhea — absence of menstruation
  • Perimenopause — the transition years before menopause, often irregular
  • Health equity — designing so outcomes don't depend on identity or access
  • Gender-inclusive design — not assuming one gendered default user

Practice question: "Is your current ICP excluding a growth segment?" Be ready to say yes — name perimenopause/menopause as the obvious expansion, and explain the product judgment (prediction models need to change, not just the marketing).

3 — Clinical Credibility & Trust-Building

When is AI-generated health guidance safe to fully automate — and when must a human, or a real doctor, be in the loop?

Best Practices

  • Define a harm model — the cost of a false positive vs. a false negative differs by prediction type
  • Involve OB/GYN or clinical advisors to review symptom-related content before shipping
  • Design explicit escalation UX for red-flag symptoms — a designed "see a doctor" moment, not a disclaimer footer
  • Never let the UI's implied confidence exceed the model's actual confidence

Vocabulary

  • Clinical validation — testing claims against real medical evidence, not just user feedback
  • Harm model — an explicit map of what happens when the system is wrong
  • Escalation path — the designed route from "AI suggestion" to "see a professional"
  • Explainability — can the user see why they got a specific recommendation

Practice question: "At what point should the product tell a user to see a doctor instead of showing a prediction?" Answer with a confidence-threshold + symptom-severity framework — not "we'd add a disclaimer."

4 — Monetization Ethics in Health

Paywalls that gate health guidance draw far more regulatory and press scrutiny than a paywall on a game level.

Best Practices

  • Never gate safety-relevant information behind a paywall
  • Cancellation should be as easy as sign-up — regulators are actively scrutinizing subscription cancellation flows
  • Transparent trial terms — no auto-renew surprises
  • Don't design monetization around health anxiety as the primary conversion lever

Vocabulary

  • Dark pattern — UI designed to manipulate rather than inform
  • ROSCA — Restore Online Shoppers' Confidence Act, governs negative-option billing in the US
  • Negative option billing — auto-renewing charges unless the user actively cancels
  • Price anchoring — showing a higher reference price to make the actual price feel smaller

Practice question: "How would you design a paywall that converts well without feeling exploitative?" Reference the anxiety-vs-value line explicitly.

AI & The Future of Product Management

Core literacy, new skills the AI era demands, vocabulary, and where to stay current

1 — Core AI Literacy Every PM Needs
  • How LLMs actually work, conceptually — next-token prediction, not a lookup or a database
  • Context window — what fits, what gets truncated, and what "context rot" means as a conversation grows long
  • Prompting vs. fine-tuning vs. RAG — three different ways to make a model "know" something, each with very different cost/speed/control tradeoffs
  • Evals — how teams measure whether an AI feature is actually good, before and after shipping
  • Latency and cost are product decisions, not just engineering constraints
  • Hallucination — probabilistic outputs will sometimes be confidently wrong; design for it, don't hope it away
2 — New Skills the AI Era Demands
  • Writing evals, not just specs — defining "good enough" for a system that's right most of the time, never all of the time
  • Running prompt/model experiments like growth experiments — hypothesis, metric, guardrail
  • Partnering with ML/applied-AI teams, not just engineering — different vocabulary, different iteration loop
  • Treating cost-per-query or cost-per-token as a live product metric, same as CAC
  • Judging build vs. buy vs. wrap — foundation model API vs. fine-tune vs. in-house model
  • Designing trust progressively — agentic features earn autonomy over time, not on day one
3 — Vocabulary / Terminology Cheat Sheet
LLM — Large Language Model
Token / Context window — units of text a model reads; the max it can hold at once
Temperature / Top-p — controls for output randomness
Prompt engineering — designing inputs to reliably steer model outputs
Fine-tuning vs. RLHF — adapting a model's weights vs. training it on human preference feedback
RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation; grounding answers in retrieved documents
Embeddings / Vector database — numeric representations of meaning; where they're stored for retrieval
Hallucination — a confident, fluent, but false output
Grounding — anchoring outputs to a verified source of truth
Eval / Evals — a structured test suite for AI feature quality
Agent / Agentic workflow — a system that plans and takes multi-step actions, not just replies
Multimodal — models that handle text, image, audio, etc. together
Inference — running a trained model to produce an output
Guardrails — rules/filters constraining what a model can output or do
System prompt — the hidden instruction set shaping a model's behavior
Few-shot / Zero-shot — giving examples in the prompt vs. none
Chain-of-thought — a model reasoning step by step before answering
Human-in-the-loop — a person reviews or approves before high-stakes actions
MCP — Model Context Protocol; a standard for connecting agents to tools and data
Context rot — quality degrading as context grows long, distinct from hitting the window limit

PRD Mastery

What it is · how to write it · two frameworks · worked example · interview Q&A

1 — What a PRD Actually Is (And Is Not)

A PRD is a document that explains: what problem we are solving, who we are solving it for, what success looks like, and what the product should do — without telling engineering how to build it. The key word is "requirements" — not solutions. Engineers decide how to build. The PM decides what needs to be true when it is done.

A PRD is NOT
✗ A feature list
✗ A technical specification
✗ A Jira ticket
✗ A design brief
A PRD IS
✓ A shared alignment document
✓ A definition of done
✓ A record of decisions made
✓ Ready when a new person can read it and understand the problem
When to write one
✓ New feature with multiple teams
✓ Complex decisions with trade-offs
✓ Stakeholder sign-off needed
✗ Small bug fixes — a ticket is enough
✗ Early spike or exploration
✗ 2-person team, 10-minute alignment
2 — The 9 Fields Every PRD Needs

Learn these 9 fields and you can write any PRD, at any company, in any format.

Field What it means + example
Problem StatementWhat is broken or missing today? Why does it matter?
"Users open the app, see no clear action, and leave within 60 seconds. There is no guided first step."
Target UserWho exactly has this problem? Be specific.
"New users in their first 7 days, especially those who signed up through a health concern like PCOS."
GoalWhat does success look like in one sentence?
"User completes a first coaching action within 3 minutes of onboarding."
Success MetricsHow will you measure it? Be specific — not "improve engagement."
"Day-1 return rate +20%. First-action completion rate >60%. D7 retention +15%."
Non-GoalsWhat are you explicitly NOT doing? Prevents scope creep.
"We are not redesigning the full dashboard. We are not adding new data sources."
User StoriesWhat does the user need to do? Format: "As a [user], I want to [action] so that [outcome]."
Keep to 3–5 key stories. Focus on the core job, not every edge case.
Key FlowsStep by step: what happens from entry to completion? Numbered list.
"1. Opens app → 2. Coaching card appears → 3. Taps action → 4. Logs completion → 5. Sees progress."
ConstraintsTechnical, legal, time, or resource limits.
"Must work offline. Must be live within 8 weeks. No new backend infrastructure in scope."
Open QuestionsWhat do you not know yet that might change the design?
"Show coaching card before or after dashboard? Personalise from Day 1 or after 3 days of data?"
3 — Two Frameworks: Classic PRD vs. Amazon PR/FAQ

Know both. Use the right one for the context.

Classic PRD
Problem-first approach
Structure: Problem → User → Goals → Metrics → Stories → Flows → Constraints → Questions
Starts from: The problem to solve
Best for: Feature work, cross-functional projects, anything needing engineering alignment
Length: 1–4 pages
✓ Use this for: Samsung Food AI coaching feature, onboarding redesign, any incremental product change
Amazon PR/FAQ
Working backwards approach
Structure: Write a fake press release as if the product already launched + FAQs answering the hardest questions
Starts from: The end result, not the problem
Best for: New products, big bets, strategy-level decisions, leadership buy-in
Length: 1 page press release + 1–2 pages FAQs
✓ Use this when: pitching a new product vision to leadership, "what are we building in 18 months?"
4 — Worked Example: Samsung Food AI Coaching Entry Point

A complete PRD written the way you'd write it at Samsung Food. Study the structure — you may be asked to write something like this live.

PRODUCT REQUIREMENTS DOCUMENT
Feature: Guided First Action — AI Nutrition Coaching Entry Point
Author: [PM Name]  |  Status: Draft  |  Date: May 2026
Problem Statement
New users who complete onboarding do not take a meaningful first action within their first session. The app presents a full dashboard with multiple entry points but no clear guided path. Users leave without experiencing the value of personalised coaching. This is the primary driver of low D1 return rate.
Target User
New users in their first 3 days. Specifically: users who completed onboarding and entered the main app for the first time.
Goal
Create a single, frictionless first coaching interaction that demonstrates personalised value before the user leaves the session.
Success Metrics
First-action completion rate: >55% of new users complete the guided action in session 1
D1 return rate: +15% vs. current baseline
D7 retention: +10% vs. current baseline
Non-Goals
We are not redesigning the main dashboard
We are not building a new AI model — we surface existing recommendations in a new entry point
We are not changing the paywall
Key User Story
"As a new user opening the app for the first time after onboarding, I want to see one clear, personalised recommendation so that I understand what the app will do for me and feel motivated to return tomorrow."
Key Flow
1. User completes onboarding → 2. Coaching card appears full-screen (not buried in dashboard) → 3. Card shows one personalised recommendation with a reason why → 4. User taps "Got it" or logs the action → 5. App confirms progress and sets expectation for tomorrow → 6. User enters main dashboard
Constraints
Must use existing recommendation engine — no new ML work
Must be live within 6 weeks
Must not require Galaxy Watch to be connected
Open Questions
Should we show this card before or after the paywall?
What happens if the recommendation engine has insufficient data to personalise yet?
Do we A/B test the card copy or ship one version first?
5 — How to Write a PRD Fast: The 20-Minute Interview Version

If they give you a whiteboard or live writing exercise — this is your process. Spend 3 minutes structuring before you write a single word.

1
Clarify (2 min)
Ask: "Who is the user? What is the business goal? Any known constraints?" Never guess — ask. This separates candidates who think from candidates who rush.
2
Problem Statement (3 min)
One or two sentences. What is broken today? For whom? Why does it matter? If you cannot write this, you do not understand the problem yet.
3
Define Success (2 min)
Two or three metrics. Specific. "Increase engagement" is not a metric. "D7 retention +15%" is.
4
Key User Story (2 min)
"As a [user], I want to [action] so that [outcome]." One sentence. This is the anchor for everything else.
5
Key Flow (5 min)

Interviewers do not expect a perfect document. They want to see how you think: do you start with the user, do you define success clearly, do you know where the scope ends? Process matters as much as output.

6 — Interview Questions About PRDs
P1
Walk me through how you write a PRD.
I always start from the problem, not the solution. Before writing a single requirement, I want to answer two questions in one sentence each: what is broken for the user today, and what does success look like when we fix it. Then I write the key user story — the anchor for every decision that follows. Then non-goals to define the boundary. Then flows and constraints. I keep PRDs short — one to two pages. If it's longer, the problem is probably not focused enough.
P2
How do you handle stakeholder disagreement on requirements?
First I find out whether the disagreement is about the requirement or about different definitions of success. Then I put the disagreement in writing — a table with Option A and Option B and the trade-offs of each. This moves from opinion to reasoning. If we still disagree, I propose a small test. An experiment with real users usually makes the decision.
P3
How do you decide what level of detail a PRD needs?
Two things: the size of the risk and the size of the team. One engineer, clear brief — a ticket is enough. Four teams, six weeks, senior stakeholder sign-off — full document. I also write more when I'm uncertain. The process of writing forces clarity. If I cannot explain the problem in two sentences, I probably don't understand it yet.
P4
What is the Amazon PR/FAQ method and when do you use it?
The Amazon method — "working backwards" — starts from the end. You write a fake press release as if the product already launched, in customer language, with a real headline and real benefits. Then you write FAQs answering the hardest questions. I use it for new product concepts or major strategic directions where leadership needs to align on vision before the team builds. For incremental feature work, a classic PRD is faster and more practical.
P5
How do you know when a PRD is good enough to hand to engineering?
Simple test: I give it to someone who was not in any planning conversations and ask them to explain back what we are building and why. If they can — it's ready. If they ask a question that should have been in the document — I add it. The goal is no surprises once engineering starts.
Vocabulary to use naturally in interviews
→ Problem statement — what is broken and for whom
→ Success metric — a specific number proving it worked
→ Non-goal — what we explicitly are not building
→ User story — "As a [user], I want [action] so that [outcome]"
→ Open question — unknown that may affect design
→ Working backwards — the Amazon PR/FAQ method

Dev Process — Agile & Sprints

What Agile is · Scrum roles · 5 ceremonies · backlog · velocity · Samsung Food scenario

1 — What Agile Is and Why It Exists
❌ Waterfall (old way)
Plan everything → Design everything → Build everything → Test → Ship

Problem: takes 6–12 months. By the time you ship, user needs have changed. You find out too late.
✓ Agile (current standard)
Ship small → Learn fast → Adjust → Repeat

Work in 2-week cycles called sprints. Every sprint ships something real. Every sprint you learn something new.

The core principle: Working software over comprehensive documentation. A feature shipped and tested beats a perfect plan on paper. Agile exists to reduce the distance between assumption and reality.

2 — Scrum Roles: Who Does What
Product Manager
You
Owns the WHAT and WHY. Writes and prioritises tickets. Defines success metrics. Accepts or rejects completed work. The voice of the user inside the team.
Tech Lead / EM
Your closest partner
Owns the HOW. Estimates effort, makes architecture decisions, protects the team from scope changes. Build trust with this person first — everything else follows.
Engineers
3–6 people typically
Build. They pull tickets from the sprint backlog — you don't assign work to individuals. They estimate their own work. Never tell an engineer how to code.
Designer
Works one sprint ahead
Works in the discovery track — one sprint ahead of engineering. Designs must be ready before a ticket enters a sprint. If design isn't ready, the ticket isn't ready.
Scrum Master
Sometimes PM does this
Facilitates ceremonies, removes blockers, protects the team's process. In small teams this is often done by the PM or tech lead — not a separate role.
3 — Sprint Anatomy: The 5 Ceremonies

A sprint = 2 weeks. These 5 meetings are the rhythm. Missing one breaks the whole cycle.

1. Sprint PlanningStart of sprint · 2 hours
PM LEADS
PM presents the top items from the backlog. Engineers estimate each ticket in story points. Team commits to what they can realistically ship this sprint — no more, no less.
Your job: Have tickets ready with designs attached and acceptance criteria written. If a ticket isn't ready, it doesn't get into the sprint.
Output: A committed sprint backlog — the team's contract for the next 2 weeks.
2. Daily StandupEvery morning · 15 minutes
TEAM OWNS
Each person answers 3 questions: What did I do yesterday? What am I doing today? Am I blocked?
Rule: This is not a status report to you. Engineers speak to each other. PM listens, notes blockers, resolves them after — not during.
Your job after standup: If someone is blocked on a PM decision (design unclear, requirement ambiguous), resolve it within the hour. Blocked engineers burn sprint capacity.
3. Refinement (Grooming)Mid-sprint · 60–90 min
MOST IMPORTANT
PM presents next sprint's tickets. Engineers ask questions. You clarify requirements, confirm designs are attached, agree acceptance criteria. Team estimates tickets.
Why it matters: Questions answered here cost nothing. Questions answered mid-sprint cost a day each. This meeting is where you prevent chaos before it happens.
Output: Tickets move to "Ready" status. Sprint planning next week becomes a 30-minute confirmation, not a 2-hour debate.
4. Sprint Review (Demo)End of sprint · 60 min
PM ACCEPTS
Engineers demo what shipped. PM and stakeholders watch. PM accepts or rejects stories against acceptance criteria.
Accepted: matches the acceptance criteria → done. Rejected: doesn't meet criteria → goes back to backlog, not "done."
What didn't ship gets discussed honestly — why, what moves, what gets cut.
Stakeholders invited: yes, especially for major features. Transparency builds trust.
5. RetrospectiveEnd of sprint · 45 min
TEAM SAFE SPACE
Three questions: What went well? What didn't go well? What do we change?
PM's posture: Listen more than talk. This is the team's space to be honest. If engineers say "tickets weren't clear" — that's feedback for you, not a complaint to deflect.
Output: 1–3 concrete action items for next sprint. Not a list of 15 things — one or two real changes.
4 — The Backlog: What It Is and How PM Manages It
Product Backlog
Owned by PM. All possible work, ordered by priority. Top = most important = most ready. The backlog is never empty and never fully done. It is a living list.
Sprint Backlog
Owned by the team. What's committed for this sprint only. PM does not add to the sprint backlog mid-sprint. The team protects this list from scope changes.

5 — Story Points & Velocity

How engineers estimate work — and how PM uses it to plan realistically.

Story Points
A relative measure of effort — not hours. Engineers estimate complexity, risk, and unknowns together. Scale: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13 (Fibonacci — the gaps reflect uncertainty).
1 pt = trivial (copy change)
2 pts = small, clear task
3 pts = medium, some unknowns
5 pts = complex, needs design
8 pts = large, break it down
13 pts = too big, split first
Velocity
Average story points completed per sprint. If your team's velocity is 32 points — don't commit 50 points in sprint planning. Velocity is your planning tool.

Never ask engineers to estimate in hours. They will always be wrong and it creates pressure. Story points exist precisely to separate effort from time.

Planning Maths (simple)
Team velocity = 30 pts/sprint  →  In a 6-sprint quarter you can commit ~180 points of work.
AI personalisation epic estimated at 80 pts  →  That's roughly 3 sprints, leaving ~100 pts for other work.
This is how PM turns a roadmap into a realistic schedule.

7 — Samsung Food Scenario: Your First Sprint as New GPM

The team already exists. They have a process. You are new. This is your playbook.

Days 1–14
Observe
Shadow every ceremony. Do not change anything yet. Read the last 10 sprints' tickets — understand their vocabulary, quality bar, and recurring blockers. Attend standups without talking.
Week 2
1-on-1s
Have individual 1-on-1s with the tech lead and each engineer. One question: "What slows you down most? What do you wish PM did differently?" Listen. Don't defend or explain. Write it down.
Week 3
Audit
Audit the backlog. Is it ordered? Do top tickets have acceptance criteria? Are designs attached? Count how many tickets are "Ready" vs. vaguely written. This tells you where the process is breaking.
Week 4
First fix
Introduce one thing: Definition of Ready checklist. Agree it with the tech lead first. Present it as "this will save everyone time" — not "the old way was wrong." Smallest intervention, highest leverage.
Sprint 2
Own it
Run your first sprint planning. Over-communicate. Write the clearest tickets the team has ever seen. For AI personalisation: have the Opportunity Brief ready, one discovery spike ticket written, and the tech lead briefed before the meeting.
Key interview answer: "How do you work with engineering teams?"
"I start by building trust before changing anything. My first two weeks I observe, ask questions, and understand how the team works. Then I focus on the highest-leverage process improvement — usually that's making sure tickets are ready before sprints start. My relationship with the tech lead is the foundation. If we're aligned on what we're building and why, everything else follows."

Epic — large feature broken into multiple stories
